# Planning Without Lying to Yourself

Product managers are often asked to do something that is quietly impossible:

> _Be honest about uncertainty — and still commit to a plan._

When organisations say they want “outcomes over outputs,” PMs are usually the first to feel the tension. Strategy demands clarity. Stakeholders expect dates. Delivery systems require sequencing. And yet the work itself — especially in new products, migrations, or architectural change — remains uncertain.

This essay is about recognising that tension for what it is, and learning how to plan _without pretending you know things you don’t_.

***

### The Pressure to Convert Uncertainty Into Certainty

Most planning pressure does not originate with PMs.\
It emerges from the wider operating system.

PMs are typically expected to:

* define scope before learning has occurred
* estimate work before the approach is proven
* sequence delivery before dependencies are resolved
* commit timelines before risks are surfaced

Over time, this creates an unspoken rule:

> _Plans must look confident, even when confidence is unjustified._

The result is not malicious deception.\
It is **institutionalised optimism**.

***

### Why Feature Roadmaps Feel Reassuring

Feature roadmaps persist because they solve a social problem.

They provide:

* legibility for stakeholders
* a sense of progress
* apparent control over complexity
* emotional reassurance under uncertainty

They are comforting because they replace _questions_ with _answers_.

But in complex product environments, those answers are often guesses — dressed up as commitments.

The roadmap becomes less a planning tool and more a coping mechanism.

***

### Known Features Do Not Equal Known Work

A common trap occurs during migrations, replacements, or platform rebuilds.

The features are familiar:

* payment flows
* integrations
* reports
* permissions
* data movement

Because the _what_ is known, the _how_ is assumed.

But new context changes everything:

* new architecture
* new constraints
* new dependencies
* new performance characteristics
* new operational risks

Knowing a feature is required does not mean you know:

* how long it will take
* what will block it
* what trade-offs will surface
* what learning will be required

Planning as if you do is not “being accountable”.

It is **lying politely to yourself and others**.

***

### Outcome Framing as a Truth-Telling Mechanism

In the Emergent Operating Model, outcomes serve a specific purpose.

They are not vague aspirations.\
They are **constraints on honesty**.

When a problem is framed as an outcome:

* assumptions become visible
* learning becomes legitimate work
* trade-offs remain open
* timelines stay provisional

This does not remove the need to deliver features.

It prevents features from being treated as promises prematurely.

Outcomes keep space for learning alive.

***

### Hypotheses Instead of Sequencing

Planning in EOM does not eliminate structure.\
It changes _what structure is used for_.

Instead of sequencing features, PMs sequence **uncertainty reduction**:

* Which assumptions are most fragile?
* Which dependencies are most risky?
* Which unknowns could invalidate the plan?

A plan becomes a **list of hypotheses to test**, not a list of things to ship.

Delivery still happens — but in service of learning, not prediction.

***

### Estimates as Signals, Not Commitments

Estimates are not forbidden.\
But they are treated differently.

In EOM-aligned planning:

* estimates express confidence ranges, not promises
* widening estimates trigger inquiry, not pressure
* prediction accuracy is not rewarded
* re-estimation is a sign of learning, not failure

The goal is not to guess better.

It is to **need guessing less over time**.

***

### The PM’s Ethical Responsibility

PMs sit at a moral fault line.

They translate between:

* strategy and execution
* uncertainty and expectation
* learning and legitimacy

Planning without lying means:

* resisting the urge to over-specify early
* naming uncertainty explicitly
* protecting learning work as real work
* challenging false certainty — especially when it is rewarded

This is not about being pessimistic.

It is about being _credible_.

***

### What Planning Looks Like When It’s Honest

Honest planning still produces artefacts.

But they look different:

* problem backlogs instead of feature backlogs
* option sets instead of fixed roadmaps
* learning milestones instead of delivery milestones
* explicit bets instead of hidden assumptions

Progress is measured by **reduced uncertainty**, not just increased output.

***

### In Closing

Planning is unavoidable.

But pretending certainty exists where it doesn’t is optional.

The Emergent Operating Model does not ask PMs to abandon responsibility.

It asks them to abandon fiction.

Planning without lying to yourself is not a softer stance.

It is a more demanding one.

Because it requires truth — even when the system prefers comfort.
